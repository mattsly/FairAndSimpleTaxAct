## Q3: Is this plan fiscally responsible?

**Yes. Revenue-neutral today, with multiple paths to long-term fiscal sustainability.**

### Short-Term: Revenue-Neutral

The Fair & Simple Tax Act is fully paid for. Revenue from the new bracket structure, capital gains reform, loophole closures, and the Great Conversion offsets the cost of FICA elimination, middle-class tax cuts, and the expanded Child Tax Credit.

We're not adding to the deficit. We're reforming *how* we collect revenue — simpler, fairer, harder to game — while holding the total constant.

### Long-Term: A Foundation for Sustainability

The US has a structural deficit driven by healthcare costs and demographics. That problem predates this bill. But the Fair & Simple Tax Act creates the foundation for solving it:

1. **A broader, simpler base.** Fewer loopholes means revenue actually materializes when rates change. Future Congresses can adjust with confidence.

2. **A system that collects what it's owed.** The tax gap — $600B+ lost annually to evasion, errors, and complexity — shrinks dramatically with automated filing and third-party reporting.

3. **Economic growth.** The most powerful fiscal policy is a growing economy. Every 1% GDP growth generates ~$50B in revenue automatically. The US is entering an AI-driven productivity boom with fundamentals stronger than any competitor. We don't need heroic assumptions — just a tax system that doesn't get in the way.

4. **Future options if needed.** A financial transaction tax, carbon pricing, or rate adjustments all become straightforward to implement on a simplified base. These aren't in this bill — different coalitions, different debates — but the foundation makes them possible.

### What We're Not Doing

We're not claiming to solve the long-term fiscal challenge in one bill. Healthcare and Social Security require separate attention. But we're building the foundation: a broad base, a functional collection system, a growing economy, and a menu of options.

**Bottom line:** Revenue-neutral now. Positioned for long-term health. No gimmicks.

## Part 2: The Core Reforms

## Q4: What happens to payroll taxes?

**For employees:** The 7.65% FICA tax disappears from your paycheck. Gone. This is an immediate raise for 170 million American workers.

**For employers:** The current 7.65% employer FICA becomes an 8% Employer Safety Net Contribution (ESNC). This funds Social Security and Medicare exactly as before — the only change is the name and a modest rate increase.

**For self-employed:** Your rate drops from 15.3% to 8%. That's a 7.3 percentage point cut — the largest tax reduction in this bill.

| Who | Old Rate | New Rate | Change |
|-----|----------|----------|--------|
| Employee | 7.65% | 0% | **-7.65%** |
| Employer | 7.65% | 8% | +0.35% |
| Self-employed | 15.3% | 8% | **-7.3%** |

**What about Social Security and Medicare?**

Fully funded. The ESNC replaces employee contributions dollar-for-dollar. Benefits don't change. Eligibility doesn't change. The only difference: workers see the money in their paychecks instead of on a pay stub deduction.

## Q5: How does the new bracket structure work?

Five brackets. Clean numbers. No phase-outs, no cliffs, no surprises.

| Single | Married Filing Jointly | Rate |
|--------|------------------------|------|
| $0 – $15K | $0 – $30K | 5% |
| $15K – $50K | $30K – $100K | 15% |
| $50K – $150K | $100K – $300K | 25% |
| $150K – $500K | $300K – $1M | 35% |
| $500K+ | $1M+ | 50% |

All thresholds are CPI-indexed annually — no more bracket creep.

The standard deduction is eliminated. Instead, the 5% bottom bracket functions as a de facto zero-tax zone for essential income, while ensuring everyone has skin in the game.

**Example:** A single filer earning $80,000 pays:
- 5% on first $15K = $750
- 15% on next $35K = $5,250
- 25% on next $30K = $7,500
- **Total: $13,500 (16.9% effective rate)**

## Q6: How are capital gains taxed?

**Simple: A lifetime exemption, then progressive rates.**

| Lifetime Capital Gains | Rate |
|------------------------|------|
| $0 – $1.5M | 0% |
| $1.5M – $3M | 15% |
| $3M – $10M | 20% |
| $10M+ | 25% |

**Per person.** When you marry, you combine your exemptions (and what's left of them).

**Why a lifetime exemption?** The $1.5M exemption replaces the primary residence exclusion — and extends it to *all* forms of wealth-building. Home sale, index funds, small business exit — same rules. Most Americans will never exceed $1.5M in lifetime gains. Those who do can afford 15-25%.

**Examples:**

| Retiree (home + stocks) | $1.2M | $0 | 0% |
| Small founder exit | $3M | $225K | 7.5% |
| Mid exit | $10M | $1.625M | 16.3% |
| Large exit | $50M | $11.625M | 23.3% |

**What we eliminated:**
- ~~Short-term vs. long-term distinction~~ (one system)
- ~~QSBS exclusions and "qualified business" definitions~~ (same rules for everyone)
- ~~Primary residence exclusion~~ (replaced by lifetime exemption)
- ~~Carried interest loophole~~ (same rules for everyone)
- ~~NIIT (3.8% surtax)~~ (eliminated)

**The user experience:** Your brokerage tracks one number — lifetime gains used. When you sell, you instantly know your rate. No holding period calculations, no "qualified" tests, no accountant required.

## Q7: What happens to my existing 401(k) or IRA?

**You have two options: convert at a discount, or keep the old rules.**

### Option 1: The Great Conversion (Recommended)

Convert your pre-tax retirement accounts (Traditional 401(k), Traditional IRA, SEP, SIMPLE) to the new American Retirement Account (ARA) at a **10% flat tax rate** — far below the 22-37% you'd pay on normal withdrawals.

**How it works:**
- You owe 10% tax on the converted balance
- Pay over 10 years, interest-free
- Converted funds now grow and withdraw tax-free (Roth-style)

**Example:** You have $500K in a Traditional 401(k).
- Conversion tax: $50,000
- Payment: $5,000/year for 10 years
- Result: $500K now in ARA, all future growth and withdrawals tax-free

**Why convert?** Under current law, you'd pay 22-37% on every dollar withdrawn. At 10%, you're locking in a massive discount — and eliminating future tax uncertainty.

### Option 2: Keep Existing Accounts

Don't want to convert? Your existing accounts remain under current rules:
- Traditional accounts: Taxed at withdrawal
- Roth accounts: Automatically become ARAs (same tax treatment)

**No new contributions** to legacy account types after the transition period. All new retirement savings go to ARA.

**Bottom line:** Convert at 10% and simplify, or keep your existing accounts under old rules. Your choice.

## Q8: What are the ARA and AOA?

Two accounts replace fifteen.

### American Retirement Account (ARA)

Your one account for retirement.

- **Contributions:** After-tax (like a Roth)
- **Growth:** Tax-free
- **Withdrawals after 59½:** Tax-free
- **Principal access:** Anytime, tax-free (like Roth)
- **Annual limit:** $60,000 (CPI-indexed)
- **Lifetime cap:** $2.5 million (CPI-indexed)
- **Replaces:** 401(k), 403(b), 457, Traditional IRA, Roth IRA, SEP-IRA, SIMPLE IRA, Solo 401(k)

### American Opportunity Account (AOA)

Your one account for life's big investments.

- **Contributions:** After-tax (like a Roth)
- **Growth:** Tax-free
- **Qualified withdrawals:** Tax-free (education, first home, starting a business, childcare, job training)
- **At age 18:** Full access for any purpose, tax-free
- **Annual limit:** $10,000 (CPI-indexed)
- **Government seed:** $1,000 at birth for every American child
- **Replaces:** 529, Coverdell ESA, ABLE accounts, Trump Accounts

### Why a $2.5M cap?

At 4% withdrawal, $2.5M provides $100K/year in tax-free retirement income — plenty for a comfortable retirement. Combined with the $1.5M lifetime capital gains exemption, a couple can shelter up to $8M in tax-advantaged wealth. That's generous for the middle class, bounded for the wealthy.

### Why all after-tax (Roth-style)?

Simpler. One set of rules. No agonizing over "Traditional vs. Roth." And it's better for most people — tax-free growth and withdrawal beats an upfront deduction, especially with decades of compounding.

**Borrowing prohibited:** Neither ARA nor AOA can be used as loan collateral.

**Bottom line:** Simple enough that everyone can use them, not just those with financial advisors.

## Q9: What is the Child Tax Credit?

**$2,500 per child, per year. Automatic. Universal. No forms.**

Every child in America with a Social Security number qualifies. The credit is applied automatically based on Social Security Administration records — no filing required, no phase-outs, no clawbacks.

| | Current Law | Fair & Simple Tax Act |
|---|-------------|----------------------|
| Amount | $2,200/child | $2,500/child |
| Filing required | Yes | No |
| Phase-outs | Yes | No |
| Refundability | Limited | Full |

**Fully refundable** means families who owe no income tax still receive the full $2,500. This isn't new — the Earned Income Tax Credit has worked this way since 1975 (signed by Gerald Ford, expanded by Ronald Reagan). We're simplifying three programs into one.

**But doesn't this mean some people pay "negative" taxes?**

Yes. A low-income family with two children might receive more in child benefits than they owe in income tax. But remember:

- We eliminated the payroll tax — previously, low-wage workers paid 7.65% from dollar one before any income tax. That burden is gone.
- Everyone pays *something* under our bracket structure — the 5% rate starts at $0, no more "zero liability" filers.
- These families still pay sales tax, property tax (in rent), and state taxes.
- The benefit goes to *children*, not as a reward for not working.

**Replaces:** Child Tax Credit (CTC), Earned Income Tax Credit (EITC), Child and Dependent Care Credit (CDCC).

**Bottom line:** One benefit, everyone gets it, supports families, no paperwork.

## Part 3: Closing Loopholes

## Q10: How do you close the "Buy-Borrow-Die" loophole?

Today, the ultra-wealthy avoid taxes with a simple playbook: buy appreciating assets, borrow against them tax-free, die and pass them to heirs with a clean slate.

We close it with two rules:

**1. Step-up eliminated.** Unrealized gains are taxed at death (see Q11).

**2. The Basis Rule.** You can borrow against any asset up to your cost basis — that's your money, you already paid tax on it. Borrowing *above* your basis triggers tax on the excess, and your basis increases by the loan amount.

**How it works:**

You bought $2M of stock, now worth $10M. You borrow $6M against it.

- Basis: $2M
- Loan: $6M
- Borrowing above basis: $4M → Taxable event
- Gain recognized: $4M (taxed at your current lifetime capital gains bracket)
- **New basis: $6M** (original $2M + $4M taxed portion)

You paid tax now, but you won't pay again on that $4M when you sell. Borrowing = selling + repurchasing, economically. No advantage either way.

**This applies to any asset:** stocks, real estate, private company shares, art, collectibles.

**What's NOT taxed:**
- Borrowing up to your basis on any asset

**What's PROHIBITED:**
- Borrowing against ARA or AOA — these accounts cannot be used as collateral

**Bottom line:** Access your invested capital anytime. Access your appreciation? Pay the tax, get the basis.

## Q11: What happens to taxes when I die?

**Your estate pays tax on unrealized gains. Your heirs inherit with a clean basis.**

Under current law, when you die, your heirs receive your assets with a "stepped-up" basis — meaning all the appreciation during your lifetime is never taxed. This is the final piece of "Buy, Borrow, Die."

**We eliminate step-up.** Unrealized capital gains are taxed on your final return, using the same lifetime brackets that apply during life.

**How it works:**

You die with $5M in stock (basis: $1M, gain: $4M).

- Lifetime exemption remaining: $1.5M (assuming you never used it)
- Taxable gain: $4M - $1.5M = $2.5M
- Tax owed: 15% on $1.5M + 20% on $1M = $425K
- Your heirs inherit stock with $5M basis (fully stepped-up after tax is paid)

**Protections for normal families:**

- **Lifetime exemption applies.** Most estates won't owe capital gains tax because gains are under $1.5M.
- **Primary residence often covered.** A home with $400K in appreciation uses exemption, not taxed.
- **Estate tax exemption.** Estates under $5M face no estate tax (just capital gains on unrealized appreciation above the lifetime exemption).
- **Liquidity provisions.** Payment plans available if estate lacks liquid assets.

**Who actually pays?**

The ultra-wealthy. Someone dying with $100M in unrealized gains will owe ~$24M in capital gains tax. That's money that was *never* going to be taxed under the old system.

**Bottom line:** You can't take it with you — and you can't pass it on tax-free anymore.

## Part 4: Common Concerns

## Q12: Won't eliminating SALT hurt middle-class families in high-tax states?

**No. We're replacing the deduction with an automatic credit — and most middle-class families come out ahead.**

The old SALT deduction was a bad deal for most Americans:

- Only ~10% of filers itemize (post-TCJA)
- The deduction was worth more to high earners (37% bracket) than middle-class families (22% bracket)
- Unlimited SALT was a federal subsidy for state tax policy choices

**What we're doing instead:**

Every filer receives an automatic SALT credit — no itemizing required. The credit is calculated using a blended rate for your state (income + sales + property taxes averaged), applied automatically.

| | Old System | Fair & Simple Tax Act |
|---|------------|----------------------|
| Who benefits | Itemizers only (~10%) | Everyone |
| Value to $50K earner | $0 (doesn't itemize) | ~$1,000-2,000 credit |
| Value to $500K earner | $10,000+ deduction | Capped credit |
| Paperwork | Track and report | Automatic |

**The broader point:**

You're also getting the FICA elimination (7.65% raise), lower brackets through $150K, and $2,500/child automatic. For a family earning $150K with two kids in New Jersey, the FICA savings alone (~$11,500) dwarf any lost SALT benefit.

**Bottom line:** We're not eliminating SALT — we're democratizing it.

## Q13: Doesn't eliminating the primary residence exclusion hurt homeowners?

**No. The lifetime exemption protects most home sales — and levels the playing field.**

Under our plan, everyone gets a **$1.5M lifetime capital gains exemption**. For most homeowners, that means their home sale is completely tax-free.

**Typical homeowner scenario:**

- Bought house in 1995 for $150,000
- Worth $550,000 today (30 years later)
- Total gain: $400,000
- Lifetime exemption used: $400,000 of $1.5M
- **Tax: $0**

The homeowner still has $1.1M of exemption remaining for future gains — stocks, a second home sale, whatever.

**Why replace the exclusion with a lifetime exemption?**

The current $250K/$500K primary residence exclusion:

- Only benefits homeowners (renters building wealth through index funds get nothing)
- Disproportionately benefits those in high-appreciation markets (luck, not merit)
- Requires tracking ownership and use tests

A renter who diligently saves $400K in a brokerage account pays capital gains tax. A homeowner with $400K in home appreciation pays nothing. Same wealth, different rules.

**The new system:** $1.5M lifetime exemption applies to *any* capital gains — home, stocks, business. Same rules for everyone. Homeowners are protected. So are renters who invested. So are small business owners.

**Bottom line:** Most home sales remain tax-free. The difference is that now everyone plays by the same rules.

## Q14: How does this affect charitable giving and nonprofits?

**We're replacing an unfair deduction with a universal credit.**

**Current system:**

- Charitable deduction only benefits itemizers (~10% of filers)
- Worth 37 cents per dollar for a billionaire, 0 cents for a middle-class family taking the standard deduction

**Our system:**

- **15% universal credit** for charitable contributions
- Available to *everyone*, not just itemizers
- Capped at 25% of tax liability

| | Old System | Fair & Simple Tax Act |
|---|------------|----------------------|
| $1,000 donation, middle-class filer | $0 benefit | $150 credit |
| $1,000 donation, wealthy filer | $370 benefit | $150 credit |

**The philosophical shift:**

The old system said: "We'll subsidize your giving more if you're rich."

The new system says: "Everyone gets the same 15 cents back per dollar donated."

**Bottom line:** Charitable giving is encouraged for everyone, not just the wealthy.

## Q15: How do our rates compare internationally?

**We're competitive with developed economies.**

Our 50% top income tax rate is above current US law (37%) but below Denmark (55.9%), France (55.4%), and Sweden (~52%). Our 25% corporate rate matches the UK and France exactly.

For capital gains, our lifetime exemption plus progressive brackets (15/20/25%) is competitive with peers. France is at 34%, Norway 38%, Denmark 42%. Most Americans will pay 0% (under the exemption) or 15-20%.

**Bottom line:** High end but not outliers. Competitive for patient capital, tougher on mega-exits.

**Comparison to Current Law:**
- Current: Step-up eliminates cap gains, $14M exemption covers estate = **$0 tax**
- Fair & Simple: **$7.5M tax**

**The heir's take:** "I'm still inheriting $12.5 million dollars. My parents paid taxes on their gains like everyone else. That's fair."

## Appendix C: Borrowing Rule Case Studies

The Borrowing Rule prevents the "Buy, Borrow, Die" strategy by treating loans against appreciated assets (above cost basis) as taxable events. Here's how it works in practice.

---

### Case 1: The Cape Cod Doctor

**Dr. Sarah Chen, 58, Primary Care Physician**

**Situation:**
- Owns vacation home in Cape Cod, bought 20 years ago for $200,000
- Current value: $1,200,000
- Unrealized gain: $1,000,000
- Wants to borrow $400,000 for daughter's medical school tuition

**Under Current Law:**
- Takes home equity loan for $400,000
- No tax triggered
- Plans to hold until death, when step-up eliminates gain
- Daughter graduates, loan gets paid off over time
- Total tax on $1M appreciation: **$0**

**Under Fair & Simple Tax Act:**

| | Amount |
|---|--------|
| Cost Basis | $200,000 |
| Loan Amount | $400,000 |
| Borrowing Above Basis | $200,000 |
| Capital Gains Tax (15% rate*) | **$30,000** |
| New Basis | $400,000 |

*Assumes she has used none of her $1.5M lifetime exemption. If fully unused, this $200K gain would be tax-free.*

**If she's already used her lifetime exemption:**
- $200,000 × 15% = $30,000 tax
- She still gets the $400,000 loan
- Her basis is now $400,000
- Remaining unrealized gain: $800,000

**Dr. Chen's take:** "I paid $30,000 in taxes to access $400,000. That's a 7.5% 'cost' — less than the interest rate on most loans. And I won't owe tax on that $200K again when I sell."

### Case 2: The Tech IPO Founder

**Marcus Williams, 42, Software Startup Founder**

**Situation:**
- Founded company 10 years ago, owns 5% post-IPO
- Shares worth $50,000,000
- Cost basis: $50,000 (original investment)
- Unrealized gain: $49,950,000
- Wants to buy a $10M house without selling shares (to avoid signaling, maintain control)

**Under Current Law:**
- Pledges $15M in stock as collateral
- Borrows $10M from private bank at 5%
- No tax triggered
- Pays interest-only, rolls loan indefinitely
- At death, step-up eliminates $50M gain
- Total tax: **$0** (plus ~$500K/year in interest)

**Under Fair & Simple Tax Act:**

| | Amount |
|---|--------|
| Cost Basis | $50,000 |
| Loan Amount | $10,000,000 |
| Borrowing Above Basis | $9,950,000 |
| Lifetime Exemption | -$1,500,000 |
| Taxable Gain | $8,450,000 |
| Tax Calculation | |
| — 15% on $1.5M ($1.5M-$3M) | $225,000 |
| — 20% on $5.45M ($3M-$8.45M) | $1,090,000 |
| **Capital Gains Tax** | **$1,315,000** |
| New Basis | $10,000,000 |

**Marcus's situation after borrowing:**
- Paid: $1,315,000 in capital gains tax
- Received: $10,000,000 loan
- Net proceeds: $8,685,000
- New basis: $10,000,000
- Remaining shares: $40M in value, $40M in unrealized gain
- Lifetime exemption: Fully used

**Marcus's take:** "I paid $1.3M to access $10M. Under the old system I'd have paid $0, but I also would have had to keep rolling debt forever. Now I own the house free and clear, my basis is reset, and I know exactly where I stand."

| | Amount |
|---|--------|

### Summary: How the Borrowing Rule Works

| Scenario | Loan | Basis | Above Basis | Tax | New Basis |
|----------|------|-------|-------------|-----|-----------|
| Cape Cod Doctor | $400K | $200K | $200K | $0-30K* | $400K |
| Tech Founder | $10M | $50K | $9.95M | $1.3M | $10M |
| Inherited Wealth | $5M | $30M | $0 | $0 | $30M |
| Serial Entrepreneur | $3M | $100K | $2.9M | $505K | $3M |
| RE Investor | $2M | $500K | $1.5M | $75K | $2M |

*Depends on remaining lifetime exemption

**Key Principles:**
1. **Borrow up to basis = no tax** (that's your money)
2. **Borrow above basis = taxable event** (that's appreciation)
3. **Basis increases by taxed amount** (no double taxation)
4. **Lifetime exemption applies** (most people pay less than headline rates)
5. **Applies to all assets** (stocks, real estate, art, private companies)

## Appendix D: Great Conversion Details

The Great Conversion offers a one-time opportunity to convert pre-tax retirement accounts to the new American Retirement Account (ARA) at a deeply discounted 10% tax rate.

---

### Why Convert?

**The Math:**

| Scenario | Tax Rate |
|----------|----------|
| Normal withdrawal (current law) | 22-37% |
| Great Conversion | **10%** |
| Savings | **12-27 percentage points** |

A $500,000 Traditional 401(k) converted today:
- Normal withdrawal tax: $110,000 - $185,000
- Great Conversion tax: $50,000
- **Savings: $60,000 - $135,000**

Plus: All future growth and withdrawals are tax-free.

---

### Eligible Accounts

**Can Convert:**
- Traditional 401(k)
- Traditional IRA
- 403(b)
- 457(b)
- SEP-IRA
- SIMPLE IRA

**Automatic Conversion (no action needed):**
- Roth 401(k) → ARA
- Roth IRA → ARA
- (Same tax treatment, just new name)

**Not Eligible:**
- HSA (remains separate)
- Taxable brokerage accounts (not retirement accounts)

---

### How It Works

**Step 1: Elect to Convert**
- 3-year enrollment window after bill passage
- Convert all or part of pre-tax balance
- Can convert in stages (Year 1: $200K, Year 2: $300K, etc.)

**Step 2: Calculate Tax**
- 10% of converted balance
- Example: Convert $400,000 → $40,000 tax

**Step 3: Payment Options**
- **Option A:** Pay in full (one-time)
- **Option B:** Pay over 10 years, interest-free
  - $40,000 tax ÷ 10 years = $4,000/year
  - Added to annual tax bill
  - No penalties for early payoff

**Step 4: Account Becomes ARA**
- Converted funds now in ARA
- All future growth tax-free
- Withdrawals after 59½ tax-free
- Subject to ARA rules ($2.5M lifetime cap)

**Consider keeping Traditional accounts if:**

3. **Need funds before 59½** — Converting requires the 10% tax; early withdrawal from Traditional may be cheaper depending on circumstances

4. **State tax considerations** — Some states don't tax retirement income; conversion may trigger state tax
